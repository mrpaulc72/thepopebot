## **Linguistic Engineering Framework**

### **Sound Symbolism Matrix**

Understanding the psychological associations of sounds is crucial for generating brand names that *feel* right. Below is a matrix of phonetic features, their linguistic class, and typical qualities they evoke, with example brand names:

| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| Plosives (p, t, k, b, d, g) | Stop consonants | Hard, decisive “burst” sounds conveying strength, impact, and often a sense of weight or authority. (In contrast to softer sounds, plosives can imply something bold or energetic.) \[VERIFIED: Klink 2000\] For example, many tech and sports brands use plosives to sound powerful or dynamic. | *Kodak*; *Pentium* (Intel’s processor name has a punchy “P” and “t” that explode) \[VERIFIED: Lexicon study\] |

### **Morphological Patterns**

To systematically generate and modify name candidates, the AI needs rules for how new names can be constructed from linguistic building blocks. Below are key morphological patterns used in professional naming, with definitions, usage contexts, and examples:

* Portmanteaus (Blended Words): This pattern creates a new name by merging parts of two (or more) words whose meanings combine to signal the brand’s concept. The resulting blend carries echoes of each source word. *When to apply:* Portmanteaus are useful when a brand positioning spans two domains or ideas and a single existing word won’t capture both. They often appear in consumer tech and media where clever combination can be memorable (and when straightforward descriptive names are taken or too generic) \[INDUSTRY STANDARD\]. *Examples:* Pinterest \= *Pin* \+ *Interest*, instantly suggesting “a collection of your interests pinned in one place.” Groupon \= *Group* \+ *Coupon*, reflecting group discounts \[VERIFIED: common examples\]. Naming agencies use blends strategically; for instance, Lexicon Branding created Febreze by blending hints of *fabric* and *breeze* to imply “a fresh breeze for fabrics” \[INFERRED from case\]. Portmanteaus allow nuanced meaning while yielding a unique, trademarkable term. \[VERIFIED: many agency portfolios contain blends\]  
* Affixation (Prefixes & Suffixes): This involves adding a familiar prefix or suffix to root words to form new names. Prefixes like “Ever-”, “Ultra-”, “Neo-” or suffixes like “-ify”, “-ly”, “-able” carry semantic weight (e.g. “newness” from *neo-*, or making active with *\-ify*). *When to apply:* Affixes are useful for signaling a specific attribute or action in a name while keeping it short. They can make a coined name feel meaningful by leveraging known linguistic pieces \[VERIFIED: Catchword glossary\]. Agencies often employ affixation when a core concept is clear (like speed, innovation, etc.) and a slight twist creates a distinctive name. *Examples:* Pentium – Lexicon chose the \-ium suffix (evoking chemical elements) to position Intel’s 5th-gen chip as fundamental and “elemental,” while the Latin root *pent-* (“five”) hints at fifth generation \[VERIFIED: Lexicon/NewYorker\]. Many modern startups add “-ify” (as in Spotify, Shopify) to imply “making X happen,” or “-ly” (as in Bitly) to create a trendy, adverb-like product name – these are deliberate morphological choices to convey function or attitude \[INFERRED\]. Similarly, Verizon is a telecom name born from *veritas* (“truth”) \+ *horizon*, using Latin roots and the “-on” ending to sound scientific yet aspirational \[VERIFIED: public case\]. Affixation rules in the AI system will allow adding/removing common morphemes so that name candidates can suggest specific meanings (e.g. prefix *“eco-*” for ecological products, suffix *“-lux”* for luxury lines) \[INFERRED\].  
* Clipping and Acronyms: Shortening words or phrases is a classic way to create succinct names. Clipping cuts a longer word/name down to one of its parts, while acronyms use initial letters. *When to apply:* This pattern is useful when an existing descriptor is too long or lacks pizzazz, but part of it is distinctive – especially in corporate or product-line contexts where brevity is valued. It also helps preserve continuity during rebrands (e.g., if a company with a generic name rebrands to its acronym). *Examples:* FedEx – clipped from *Federal Express* to a punchy two-syllable name that feels fast \[VERIFIED: common knowledge\]. Cisco – derived from *San Francisco*, retaining a hint of its origin while standing alone as a name \[INFERRED\]. IBM – an acronym for *International Business Machines*, which by contracting to initials became ownable and modern \[INDUSTRY STANDARD\]. Naming agencies will recommend truncation when a straightforward descriptor or legacy name needs energizing. For instance, a hypothetical “Advanced Analytics Corporation” might become “Anacorp” or “AAC” if a shorter form is more brand-friendly. The AI system’s morphological rules will include abbreviation logic (e.g., generating acronym options for multi-word inputs, or clipping at natural breakpoints) with the caveat that the results must still be distinctive and easy to say. *(Note:* Acronyms that just describe a category (like “TBC” for “The Bike Company”) are usually avoided unless the acronym itself is catchy, because they often lack personality \[INDUSTRY STANDARD\].)  
* Invented Coinages (Neologisms): Completely made-up words that are not found in any dictionary. These may be built from syllables or phonemes chosen for their sound quality or vague resemblance to meaningful roots, but they have no direct semantic meaning to consumers initially. *When to apply:* Agencies turn to fully invented names when trademark space is crowded or when a brand needs a totally unique “empty vessel” to fill with its own meaning. This approach is common in highly saturated industries (automotive, pharma, tech) and for companies aiming to create a new category. *Examples:* Kodak – famously coined by George Eastman with no meaning, chosen for its short, punchy sound and the letter “K” which he felt was strong \[VERIFIED: historical anecdote\]. Xerox – an invented name based on the xerography technology, but essentially a new word that became synonymous with photocopying \[INDUSTRY STANDARD\]. Lexicon’s work on Sonos (the home audio brand) produced a short palindromic name that feels musical but isn’t a dictionary word – an empty vessel suggesting “sound” indirectly (echoing *sonus*, Latin for sound) while being globally neutral \[INFERRED from pattern\]. Invented names must be *phonetically appealing* and easy to pronounce, or they risk feeling alien. Agencies often do extensive linguistic checks on coined candidates to ensure they carry no hidden bad meanings and resonate positively by sound alone \[VERIFIED: New Yorker – Lexicon employs linguists worldwide to vet invented names\]. One pitfall: just because a coined name can be broken into suggestive bits doesn’t mean it works – as Igor Naming warns, “having a meaning doesn’t make a name meaningful.” A name like *“Mirvie”* was rationalized with bits of Latin and French by one agency, but to the target audience it’s meaningless \[VERIFIED: Igor Naming guide\]. Our AI system will generate neologisms by recombining phonemes and using sound-symbolism rules, but will flag if a made-up name lacks any strategic association – ensuring we don’t propose gibberish that only *sounds* pseudo-meaningful. \[INFERRED\]

*(Verification Note: Portmanteau and affix usage are well documented in agency case studies and naming literature \[VERIFIED\]. Direct proprietary rules for how firms coin names are not published – agencies guard these methods – so the above principles are distilled from known examples and general linguistic strategy \[INFERRED\].)*

### **Processing Fluency Factors**

Why do some names “stick” in memory and roll off the tongue, while others feel clumsy? The AI naming system must evaluate candidates for processing fluency, i.e. how easily a person can perceive, pronounce, and remember a name. Key factors influencing a name’s cognitive and linguistic fluency include:

* Name Length & Syllable Count: In general, shorter names (ideally 2-3 syllables or less) are easier to process and recall \[INDUSTRY STANDARD\]. Many top brands have two syllables (Ap-ple, Goo-gle) or even one (Sprint, Zoom). Long or compound names risk being shortened by the public anyway (e.g. “FedEx” for Federal Express) \[INDUSTRY STANDARD\]. That said, ultra-short names must still be distinctive (e.g. *Tesla* is two syllables but unique). The system will favor concise names for memorability, unless the brief calls for a multisyllabic style (some law firms or nonprofits intentionally use longer names). If a name exceeds \~3 syllables or 10 characters, we flag it as potentially cumbersome \[INFERRED\].  
* Pronounceability and Phonetic Clarity: A name should be easy to say for the target audience. This means avoiding awkward consonant clusters or tongue-twister sequences, and respecting phonological rules of key languages. For example, *“Xyqtal”* might look distinctive but would stump English speakers with “xyq-” upfront \[INFERRED\]. Agencies perform pronunciation checks in multiple languages to ensure a name that’s easy in one tongue isn’t impossible in another (e.g. *“Luxeon”* might be fine in English, but “lux” can be hard for East Asian speakers without an ending vowel) \[VERIFIED: Catchword interview – linguistics screening identifies difficult clusters\]. The AI will simulate pronunciation for different language phonologies and downgrade names with likely pronunciation issues or that require spelling clarification when heard. A fluent name typically has a straightforward vowel-consonant pattern that most people can predict when reading (or conversely, can spell after hearing). Simplicity in sound structure contributes to fluency. As one agency principal noted, *“Our brains don’t like complex ideas… if your brand name is too complex, people will move past it.”* \[VERIFIED: Lexicon’s Kennedy Placek\]  
* Distinctiveness and Visual Rhythm: A name that’s too similar to common words or competitors can be processed fluently (because it’s familiar) but then easily forgotten or confused. There’s a balance between familiarity and novelty. The ideal name is *just familiar enough* in structure to be comfortable, but novel enough to grab attention \[INFERRED\]. Additionally, the visual design of a name (letters and length) plays a role in memory: short names or those with symmetrical or repetitious letters (like “SOS”, “Civic”, or “Sonos”) can be easier to recall because they form distinct visual patterns \[INFERRED\]. While visual potential isn’t a linguistic factor per se, agencies consider whether a name looks clean and balanced in a logo or URL. For instance, palindrome names like *“Level”* or repeating-letter names like *“Google”* (with its double ‘o’) have visual symmetry that reinforces recall \[INFERRED\]. The AI will evaluate names for any awkward character sequences (like multiple consecutive X, or alternating caps if stylized) and prefer names that are visually simple and easy to typeset. This ensures chosen names are not only sayable but also *design-friendly* for logos and app icons. \[INFERRED\]  
* Memory Aids (Repetition, Alliteration, Rhyme): Names that employ rhetorical sound devices tend to stick in memory. Repetition is a proven booster of liking and recall – e.g., brand names with repeated sounds or syllables like Coca-Cola, M\&M’s, TikTok are processed more fluently and often viewed favorably \[VERIFIED: Argo, *Journal of Marketing* 2010 study on sound repetition\]. Alliteration (same starting sound, as in PayPal, Best Buy) or rhyme (as in FireWire) creates a poetic cadence that makes names more memorable and satisfying to the brain. Our system will incorporate checks for such devices: a candidate that naturally contains repeating phonemes or a melodic pattern might score higher on the memorability dimension \[INFERRED\]. However, it will also consider subtlety – a name shouldn’t sacrifice meaning just to rhyme. The goal is to use repetition or alliteration opportunistically to increase fluency, in line with research that consumers respond positively to phonetic harmony in a name \[VERIFIED: industry research\].  
* Cross-Linguistic Compatibility: Fluency extends beyond English. A name that is easy in one culture might be hard or carry different meanings elsewhere. The AI will integrate a multilingual profanity and unpleasant-meaning filter (for major markets) \[VERIFIED: Tanj guide – agencies do linguistic checks across geographies\]. For example, an English name ending in “-us” might sound like a Latin noun (fine in West) but in Russian that ending could form a masculine noun that might be awkward; or a name with “Po” could be harmless in the US but in French “po” starts vulgar slang. These nuances affect whether a name is smoothly adoptable globally. Professional namers use worldwide linguist networks to vet such issues early \[VERIFIED: Lexicon uses 77+ linguists worldwide\]. The AI system will similarly flag low processing fluency for any language group where the name becomes a tongue-twister or a negative word. A truly fluent name is one that, after basic introduction, *feels natural* to say in the target languages – which greatly aids word-of-mouth spread and brand recall. \[VERIFIED: industry practice\]

*Verification:* Principles above draw from both peer-reviewed consumer research (on sound repetition, vowel effects) \[VERIFIED\] and common industry practices (e.g. the “7–2 rule” many namers use: a great name is ideally 7 letters or fewer, 2 syllables or fewer) \[INDUSTRY STANDARD\]. Not every great name follows every rule (e.g. “Banana Republic” is 6 syllables yet succeeded \[INDUSTRY EXCEPTION\]), but the AI will use these factors as weighted criteria to rank names that are easy to process and remember. (For instance, a name might be scored out of 100 with \~30% weight to linguistic ease, \~25% to length/memory factors, etc., in line with their importance as gleaned from agency methodologies \[INFERRED\].)

## **Naming Taxonomy & Decision Framework**

### **Name Type Taxonomy**

Professional naming agencies classify name ideas into clear categories. This helps match the *type* of name to a client’s strategic needs and also signals the level of legal protection likely. Below is a unified taxonomy of name types synthesized from multiple agency frameworks \[VERIFIED: How Brands Are Built, Igor guide\]. Each category includes its definition, typical use cases, trademark implications, and examples:

* Descriptive Names: Names that directly describe the product, service, or its key attribute. These leave little to imagination – they tell you *what it is*. *Use cases:* Descriptive names are chosen for clarity and immediate understanding, often in conservative industries or for new product categories where the function needs to be explicit. Startups on a tight marketing budget might use a descriptive name so customers “get it” instantly (e.g., The Weather Channel, Hotels.com). *Trademark implications:* Descriptive names are the weakest in terms of legal protection – in many jurisdictions they cannot be trademarked unless they acquire distinctiveness over time, because they merely describe a category \[VERIFIED: USPTO guidelines\]. For example, “Shredded Wheat” was so descriptive of the cereal that the courts deemed it generic. Agencies typically avoid purely descriptive names if possible, or they’ll advise coupling them with a distinctive modifier. *Examples:* Crunchy granola (fictional example) for a cereal – self-descriptive; General Motors for a car company – broad and descriptive. These names work best when you need no-nonsense communication, but they require heavy marketing to stand out. Many times, descriptive names get abbreviated or turned into acronyms over time due to their length or blandness (e.g. “International Business Machines” → IBM).  
* Suggestive / Evocative Names: Names that hint at a benefit, metaphor, or emotion related to the brand, without directly stating the product. They work by association or analogy. *Use cases:* Suggestive names are popular for consumer brands and any scenario where differentiation is important. They strike a balance between being meaningful and being unique. An evocative name can capture the brand’s essence or story (e.g. Virgin for an airline – implies freshness and rebellion against industry norms) \[VERIFIED: Igor interview\]. Agencies recommend suggestive names when the brand positioning can be summed up in a concept or feeling rather than a literal product description. *Trademark:* These are stronger than descriptive names – suggestive marks are generally protectable since they require consumer imagination to connect to the product. (They’re not outright descriptive.) However, if the suggestion is very obvious, there can be borderline cases. *Examples:* Netflix – suggests “internet \+ flicks (movies)”, evoking the idea of streaming entertainment without describing it bluntly. Greyhound (bus service) – metaphorically suggests speed and sleekness of a greyhound dog, instead of literally “Fast Bus Lines.” Amazon (online marketplace) – an evocative use of the Amazon jungle’s vastness to imply huge selection \[INFERRED\]. These names often come from creative exercises linking product attributes to images or concepts. Many naming firms have frameworks to generate evocative names; Igor, for instance, champions moving from functional naming toward evocative, stating that *evocative names “evoke the positioning” of a company rather than describe it* \[VERIFIED: Igor Naming Guide\].  
* Abstract / Coined Names: Names with no pre-existing meaning in relation to the product – often completely invented words (fanciful names) or real words used out-of-context. They are also called “empty vessel” names. *Use cases:* Used when a unique identity and strong trademark is a priority. Tech companies and pharmaceuticals often use coined names (e.g. Xerox, Zynga) to ensure exclusivity. Abstract names can also future-proof a brand that may extend into diverse categories (since the name isn’t tied to one thing). *Trademark:* Fanciful coined names are the strongest category for trademark protection – they’re inherently distinct. (E.g. Kodak or Exxon had no meaning before their use, so virtually any use is infringing.) Real words used arbitrarily (like Apple for electronics) are also highly protectable since “apple” has no relevance to computers (these are known legally as “arbitrary” marks, a subset of abstract names). *Examples:* Google – a coined play on “googol” suggesting huge numbers, but effectively an abstract term that became filled with meaning. Uber – a real word (“uber” meaning super/extreme) but chosen out-of-context for a rideshare, now purely a brand token. Kodak – a made-up word designed to be short and memorable \[VERIFIED: Kodak history\]. Abstract names rely on sound and brand narrative to imbue meaning after launch – they typically require more marketing to educate consumers. Agencies pursue abstract names when differentiation trumps immediate comprehension and when the brand has resources to build an image from scratch. Lexicon Branding, for example, often creates coined names backed by linguistic research to ensure they “feel right” (like “Swiffer” which was novel but evoked swift cleaning) \[VERIFIED: Lexicon case\].  
* Founder Names: Eponymous brands named after the person or family who founded the company or product. *Use cases:* Common in industries where personal reputation or heritage matters – fashion (e.g. Chanel, Ford (cars) for Henry Ford), consulting firms, luxury goods, and some food/beverage brands. Using a founder’s name can signal trust, craftsmanship, or legacy. It’s also a straightforward choice when a founder is already well known (e.g. Disney was named after Walt Disney, leveraging his fame \[INDUSTRY STANDARD\]). *Trademark:* Founder names can be trademarked if they acquire distinctiveness, but protection may be limited if the name is a common first or last name. There is also risk if others share that name. Still, many founder names become strong trademarks (Ford®, Dell®, etc.) because they come to identify one source. One challenge: founder names are less flexible if the brand’s focus changes (unless the founder’s persona is broad). *Examples:* Disney, Toyota (from founder Kiichiro Toyoda, with a slight spelling tweak), Ben & Jerry’s ice cream (named for its two founders). Agencies might suggest a founder name when authenticity and story are key selling points, or if the founder’s story is central to the brand identity. However, modern practice often pairs a founder name with a descriptive or evocative element for clarity (e.g. Wendy’s – founder’s nickname – plus “Old Fashioned Hamburgers” as descriptor in the logo).  
* Geographic Names: Names that reference a place or region. This could be the company’s origin or an aspirational locale that conveys a certain image. *Use cases:* Often used for foods, beverages, apparel, or any brand wanting to evoke the qualities associated with a place (e.g., *savannah* for natural feel, *Paris* for elegance). Also employed when regional pride or provenance is a key differentiator (e.g. California Pizza Kitchen). Some legacy corporate names use geography simply because that’s where they started (e.g. Bank of America). *Trademark:* Geographic terms on their own are generally not protectable if they are literally descriptive of origin (you couldn’t trademark “Atlanta Brewing Co.” if based in Atlanta without difficulty). However, if the geography is used metaphorically or the place has no direct link to the product, it can function as a trademark (e.g. Amazon as a name for an internet company – Amazon is a place, but it’s arbitrary/unrelated to e-commerce, making it protectable and evocative). Many luxury brands use exotic place names for cachet (e.g. Acura chose the name “Integra” for a car model, not a place but an example of an arbitrary Latin/Euro word to sound international). Geographic names that aren’t the actual origin can still carry risk of being seen as misdescriptive if consumers are misled. *Examples:* Patagonia (outdoor clothing – named after the rugged Patagonian region to evoke adventure \[INDUSTRY STANDARD\]); Arizona Iced Tea (evokes a hot, sun-drenched climate, though the company started in NYC – the place name sets a mood). Agencies will leverage geographic names when a location’s mythos aligns with brand identity, but they’ll usually check that the name isn’t overused or generic in that space (e.g. countless brands have used “Pacific” or “Atlantic” in names, diminishing distinctiveness).  
* Acronyms & Initialisms: Names formed from the initials or abbreviations of longer names or phrases. (Strictly speaking, acronyms form a pronounceable word like NASA, whereas initialisms are spoken as letters like IBM.) *Use cases:* Often born from necessity – either a cumbersome descriptive name or a previous multi-word name that needs shortening. Common in older corporations and government (when literal names were the norm historically). Today, some brands intentionally create an acronym to appear modern or technical (e.g. SK-II skincare, or HSBC bank). Acronyms are also used when companies merge or want to carry forward some brand equity from an old name in a new, sleeker form. *Trademark:* Acronyms can be strong marks if the letters themselves aren’t generic. However, if the acronym is basically shorthand for a descriptive phrase, it might initially be seen as descriptive as well (e.g. “NFL” stands for National Football League – each word is generic, but NFL has become a protected mark through usage). The more an acronym looks and sounds like a unique term (like “IKEA”, which comes from founder’s initials \+ farm name, yet is pronounced as a name), the stronger legally. *Examples:* IBM (from a highly descriptive original name – now a distinct brand on its own); UPS (United Parcel Service – the acronym became the brand, with a slogan “What can Brown do for you?” leveraging UPS’s nickname “Brown”). Modern agencies rarely propose brand-new acronyms that no one is using, because random letters are hard for consumers to remember unless they already have meaning. But acronyms are still relevant in brand architecture (e.g. a long product series name might be abbreviated internally). When working with acronyms, naming experts ensure they aren’t accidentally a negative word (e.g. “Federated Unified Corp” probably shouldn’t go by FUC\!). Our AI will generate acronyms sparingly – mainly as options if input names or phrases lend themselves to a catchy sequence of letters \[INDUSTRY STANDARD\].

*(Verification: The above taxonomy is confirmed by multiple sources – e.g., a branding article notes that “evocative (suggestive) vs. functional (descriptive)” is a key spectrum and that coined vs real-word is another axis. The USPTO’s definitions of arbitrary vs fanciful marks also align with the abstract/coined category. This framework therefore represents verified categories used by top agencies, including Igor’s distinctions and others’ terminologies. \[VERIFIED\])*

### **Strategic Selection Framework**

Deciding what type of name best suits a brand is a strategic choice. Naming agencies use frameworks and criteria to guide this decision, ensuring the chosen name style aligns with brand strategy and market context. Key decision factors include:

* Industry Conventions and Differentiation: Agencies first examine the naming landscape of the product’s industry. If all competitors use a particular style (say, acronyms in finance or descriptive in pharmaceuticals), the strategist asks whether to conform for clarity or break the pattern to stand out. For example, in airlines, names historically were literal (American, United) until Virgin Atlantic came along as an evocative break-the-mold name \[VERIFIED: Igor interview\]. Breaking industry naming norms can make a name more memorable (Igor’s Steve Manning calls this “finding a fresh way in” to customers’ hearts) \[VERIFIED: Igor\]. Conversely, in some categories (like medical devices sold to conservative buyers), a bizarre coined name might impede trust, so a more suggestive or descriptive name might be strategically chosen. The AI system will assess competitor names (input as data) and flag opportunities to differentiate. For instance, if competitors have dull functional names, a more evocative name could be a strategic advantage \[INFERRED\]. Agencies often explicitly plot names on an axis from common to novel during strategy phase, to visualize this differentiation.  
* Brand Positioning and Story Alignment: The naming team looks at the brand’s core positioning (the key idea or promise). Different name types serve different positioning strategies. If the positioning is highly emotional or metaphorical, an evocative name will best amplify that. (E.g., a car positioned around freedom might get a name like “Outback” or “Mustang” – suggestive of exploration or power \[INFERRED\].) If the positioning is tightly linked to a new technology or process, a descriptive or invented name could lend credibility (e.g., a novel tech ingredient might use a coined scientific-sounding name to sound advanced). Igor’s naming guide emphasizes aligning name style with positioning: a name can be Functional, Invented, Experiential, or Evocative, and that choice depends on what aspect of the brand you want to foreground \[VERIFIED: Igor guide\]. *Experiential* names (Igor’s term for names that refer to an experience or benefit, like Safari for Apple’s browser) fall in between descriptive and purely evocative – these are chosen when user experience is the focus. The framework essentially asks: “Does this brand win by being straightforward, by painting a picture, or by creating a new word we fill with meaning?” \[INFERRED\]. Our system will use the brand brief inputs (desired brand personality, values, and customer promise) to score which name category aligns best. For example, if a brief stresses “we are a disruptive, fun brand” and competitors are stodgy, the system leans toward abstract or playful suggestive names. If the brief is “we need to build initial trust in a new category,” it might lean a bit more descriptive or use real words that carry credibility \[INFERRED\].  
* Brand Architecture & Extension Considerations: The selection must account for how the name will fit into or enable a broader naming architecture. Agencies decide name types partly by looking at the company’s portfolio and future plans. If this is a corporate name that will later endorse sub-brands, it might need to be more abstract or short (to combine well with descriptors in product names). Conversely, a very category-specific name could box the brand in if it expands. (E.g., a company named “Acme Software Backup Inc.” struggled when it started offering more than backup services \[UNVERIFIED illustrative\].) Catchword’s experts note you must *“plan for the future and anticipate shifts – don’t pigeonhole the product with too narrow a name.”* \[VERIFIED: Catchword interview\] Thus, agencies might favor an empty vessel/coined name when long-term flexibility is a priority – you can extend a brand like “Uber” from cars to food delivery, but a name like “CityTaxiApp” would have limited it. On the other hand, in a house of brands strategy, each product might get a highly specific evocative name while the corporate name remains abstract. Our AI will incorporate tags for how extensible a name is – e.g., does it reference a specific product or attribute that might change? – and can recommend more general abstract names for master brand situations vs. descriptive names for stand-alone products that won’t diversify \[INFERRED\].  
* Target Audience and Cultural Context: The “right” name type also hinges on who the name is for. Agencies consider the linguistic preferences and expectations of the target demographic. A playful coined name might delight a younger consumer base but confuse or alienate an older or international audience if it sounds too unfamiliar. For instance, a B2B enterprise software for conservative clients might avoid whimsical names and lean towards suggestive or acronym names that sound established (many cybersecurity firms use coined Latin or compound names that sound serious). In contrast, a Gen-Z focused app can use quirky, invented spelling (e.g. “Tumblr” deliberately misspelled, or “Spotify” with its playful suffix) \[INFERRED\]. Cultural factors matter: in some languages, certain phonetic styles are viewed as elegant vs. cheap. Agencies often gather feedback from regional market experts in early strategy to sense what type of name will resonate or be pronounceable locally \[VERIFIED: Tanj – culturally sensitive naming\]. For example, an English pun name might not travel globally, so a more universal abstract name could be chosen for worldwide launches. Overall, Gogo demonstrates the payoff of creative courage and alignment with user experience (internet in flight was exciting, the name feels exciting). Igor’s success here likely influenced other tech naming, reinforcing that sometimes the simplest, most upbeat name is the smartest.
