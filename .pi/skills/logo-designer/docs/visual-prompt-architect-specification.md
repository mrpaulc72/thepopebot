### AUDIENCE ALIGNMENT

**Visual Devices for Young/Millennial Audiences**
- Hand-drawn illustration style with expressive linework
- Saturated, bold colors with contemporary confidence
- Playful mascots with extended personality
- Dynamic, asymmetric composition
- Approachable, friendly typography
- Reference: Buttermilk Creative (Mortal Kombucha), Gander (Yellowbird), Happyland Creative

**Visual Devices for Premium/Affluent Audiences**
- Sophisticated typography (serif, custom refined sans-serif)
- Restrained color palettes with strategic use of accent colors
- High-quality illustration with professional refinement
- Clean, hierarchical composition with generous white space
- Refined materials and finish (emboss, texture effects)
- Reference: Garage Design (Huel), Gander (Graza premium positioning), Eye Candy (corporate clients)

**Visual Devices for Family/Mainstream Audiences**
- Clear, readable typography
- Warm, accessible color palettes
- Professional product photography
- Simple, understandable messaging
- Friendly but not overly playful visual language
- Reference: My Better Batch (busy moms 30-50), various CPG brands

**Visual Devices for Health-Conscious Audiences**
- Natural color palettes (greens, earth tones, whites)
- Clean, minimal composition
- Transparent ingredient information
- Professional, trustworthy presentation
- Often combined with playful elements to prevent "medicinal" feel
- Reference: Eye Candy's health & wellness specialty

### PALETTE TYPES WITH DEFINITIONS

**Monochromatic**
- Definition: Single hue with varying tones and shades (white to black)
- When to Use: Premium, sophisticated, minimal positioning; strong brand focus; elegance emphasis
- Brand Examples: Apple (spatial), certain luxury brands
- Prompt Language: "Monochromatic palette," "single hue variations," "tonal variations of [color]"

**Complementary**
- Definition: Two colors opposite on color wheel (red-green, blue-orange, yellow-purple)
- When to Use: High contrast, maximum shelf differentiation, vibrant positioning
- Brand Examples: Gander/Yellowbird (yellow + purple variant sublabels), Graza (green + peachy-sand)
- Prompt Language: "Complementary color contrast," "color wheel opposites," "high-contrast palette"

**Analogous**
- Definition: Colors adjacent on color wheel (red-orange-yellow, blue-green-teal)
- When to Use: Harmonious, cohesive positioning, sophisticated color relationships
- Brand Examples: Graza (various green tones + peachy yellow)
- Prompt Language: "Analogous color harmony," "adjacent color wheel palette," "harmonious color relationship"

**Triadic**
- Definition: Three colors equally spaced on color wheel (forms triangle)
- When to Use: Balanced vibrancy, contemporary positioning, visual interest
- When to Avoid: Can appear chaotic if not carefully balanced
- Prompt Language: "Triadic color scheme," "balanced three-color palette"

**Split-Complementary**
- Definition: Base color + two colors adjacent to its complement
- When to Use: Vibrant but less jarring than full complementary
- Brand Examples: Color schemes balancing contrast with harmony
- Prompt Language: "Split-complementary palette," "complex color balance"

**Neutral-Plus-Accent**
- Definition: Neutral palette (white, black, gray, beige) with single accent color
- When to Use: Premium positioning, minimalism, focus on accent for key information
- Brand Examples: Huel (minimal + accent), many luxury brands
- Prompt Language: "Neutral palette with [accent color]," "minimalist with accent," "restrained with strategic color"

## SECTION 3.2: TYPOGRAPHY VOCABULARY

### TYPE CLASSIFICATIONS WITH CHARACTERISTICS

**Geometric Sans-Serif**
- Characteristics: Based on perfect geometric forms (circles, squares, triangles); even stroke weight; minimal personality
- Personality: Modern, clean, contemporary, technical, minimalist
- When to Use: Tech brands, contemporary positioning, minimal aesthetic
- Legibility: Excellent at large sizes; can lose readability at small sizes due to geometric purity
- Examples: Futura, Avenir
- Prompt Language: "Geometric sans-serif," "clean geometric forms," "contemporary minimalism," "technical clarity"

**Humanist Sans-Serif**
- Characteristics: Based on calligraphic forms; varied stroke weight; more personality; open letters
- Personality: Warm, friendly, approachable, human, contemporary
- When to Use: Approachable brands, wellness, contemporary accessibility
- Legibility: Excellent, warm feel at all sizes
- Examples: Gill Sans, Calibre
- Prompt Language: "Humanist sans-serif," "warm approachable," "friendly contemporary," "open letterforms"

**Grotesque Sans-Serif**
- Characteristics: 19th-century origins; strong character; geometric but not pure; authoritative
- Personality: Bold, confident, strong, historical, assertive
- When to Use: Bold positioning, strong brands, confidence
- Examples: Franklin Gothic, Univers
- Prompt Language: "Grotesque sans-serif," "bold confident," "strong assertive," "historical power"

**Modern Serif**
- Characteristics: Thin to thick stroke contrast; geometric serifs; contemporary approach
- Personality: Sophisticated, contemporary, luxury, refined
- When to Use: Premium positioning, sophisticated brands, luxury
- Examples: Didot, Bodoni
- Prompt Language: "Modern serif," "sophisticated luxury," "refined contrast," "contemporary elegance"

**Transitional Serif**
- Characteristics: Bridge between Old Style and Modern; moderate contrast; timeless quality
- Personality: Classical, timeless, sophisticated, balanced, approachable
- When to Use: Heritage brands, classic positioning, sophisticated approachability
- Examples: Garamond, Times New Roman
- Prompt Language: "Transitional serif," "timeless elegance," "classical balance," "heritage sophistication"

**Slab Serif**
- Characteristics: Heavy, block-like serifs; strong personality; modern yet classic feel
- Personality: Bold, confident, contemporary, creative, strong
- When to Use: Bold contemporary brands, creative positioning, strong identity
- Examples: Rockwell, Clarendon
- Prompt Language: "Slab serif," "bold contemporary," "creative strength," "confident personality"

**Script/Cursive**
- Characteristics: Connected letterforms mimicking handwriting; flowing, elegant
- Personality: Elegant, feminine, formal, personal, sophisticated
- When to Use: Premium positioning, feminine brands, formal events, personal connection
- Legibility Issues: Can be difficult to read at small sizes; should be used sparingly
- Examples: Brush Script, Edwardian Script
- Prompt Language: "Script lettering," "elegant flowing," "handwritten feel," "formal grace"

**Display/Decorative**
- Characteristics: Highly stylized; unique personality; attention-grabbing
- Personality: Theatrical, attention-getting, unique, experimental, creative
- When to Use: Headings, posters, distinctive brand marks; rarely for body copy
- Legibility: Often sacrifices legibility for visual impact
- Examples: Varies widely (Playfair, Abril, etc.)
- Prompt Language: "Display typeface," "decorative character," "theatrical attention," "unique personality"

**Hand-Lettered/Custom**
- Characteristics: Drawn by hand (digitized); unique, personal, imperfect
- Personality: Authentic, warm, personal, artisanal, unique, human
- When to Use: Premium artisanal brands, authentic positioning, personal connection
- Legibility: Depends on execution; varies from excellent to challenging
- Examples: Custom drawn letterforms specific to brand
- Prompt Language: "Hand-lettered," "custom letterforms," "artisanal warmth," "personal authentic," "drawn type"

**Custom/Bespoke**
- Characteristics: Specially created for specific brand; unique to brand identity
- Personality: Exclusive, distinctive, premium, branded, memorable
- When to Use: Premium brands, distinctive positioning, high-budget branding
- Examples: Garage Design's custom letterforms, Gander's Spanish-inspired type
- Prompt Language: "Custom lettering," "exclusive typeface," "bespoke typography," "distinctive letterforms"

## SECTION 3.3: COMPOSITION VOCABULARY

### LAYOUT PATTERNS

**Horizontal Lockup**
- Description: Logo elements arranged left-to-right; text and symbol aligned horizontally
- When to Use: Websites, business cards, horizontal applications, standard packaging label position
- Advantages: Natural reading flow, space-efficient horizontally
- Disadvantages: Can appear stretched at certain scales
- Prompt Language: "Horizontal lockup," "side-by-side arrangement," "left-to-right composition"

**Vertical/Stacked Lockup**
- Description: Logo elements stacked top-to-bottom; symbol above text or vice versa
- When to Use: Square formats, vertical applications, packaging sides, narrow spaces
- Advantages: Works well on narrow vertical spaces, flexible scaling
- Disadvantages: Requires adequate vertical space
- Prompt Language: "Vertical stacked lockup," "top-to-bottom arrangement," "stacked composition"

**Centered Symmetrical**
- Description: Elements arranged symmetrically around vertical center axis
- When to Use: Classic, formal, balanced positioning; emblems, seals
- Personality: Formal, classical, balanced, traditional
- Prompt Language: "Centered symmetrical," "balanced symmetry," "formal centered composition"

- When to Use: Contemporary, dynamic, playful brands; modern positioning
- Personality: Contemporary, energetic, dynamic, modern, playful
- Prompt Language: "Asymmetric balance," "dynamic composition," "contemporary arrangement," "off-center balance"

**Contained (In Shape)**
- Description: Logo elements contained within geometric or organic shape (circle, square, hexagon, badge shape)
- When to Use: App icons, badges, emblem formats, cohesive marks
- Advantages: Self-contained, distinctive silhouette, strong mark
- Prompt Language: "Contained within shape," "badge format," "geometric container," "enclosed composition"

- Prompt Language: "Badge emblem," "circular border," "classic heraldic," "ornate frame"
