## **II. SOUND SYMBOLISM: SCIENTIFICALLY VERIFIED**

## **Academic Foundation**

Yorkston & Menon (2004) — Journal of Consumer Research

Two controlled studies (N=237 total) demonstrated that sound symbolism:

* Operates automatically (uncontrollable, outside awareness, effortless)  
* Influences attribute perceptions (front vowels \= small/light; back vowels \= large/heavy)  
* Persists under cognitive load (effects stronger when consumers distracted)  
* Occurs below consciousness (consumers unaware of influence; F=9.51, p\<.01)

Methodology:

* Manipulated single vowel: /ɑ/ vs. /i/ in fictitious ice cream names (Frosh vs. Frish)  
* Measured smoothness, creaminess, richness ratings  
* Found 30%+ evaluation difference driven by phonetics alone

Klink (2000) — Food Quality Research

Demonstrated cross-category phonetic effects:

* Front vowels (small-sounding) → "light, delicate, thin" perceptions  
* Back vowels (large-sounding) → "heavy, rich, substantial" perceptions

## **III. VERIFIED CASE STUDY ANALYSIS**

## **PENTIUM (Intel, 1999): Sound-Symbolism Engineering**

Strategic Brief:

* Problem: Processor numbering (486, 586\) indistinguishable from competitors  
* Opportunity: Create branded differentiation  
* Execution: Phonetic engineering to convey "power \+ scientific credibility"

Phonetic Breakdown:

| Phoneme | Property | Effect |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| /p/ | Voiceless plosive | POWER, impact, strength |
| /ɛ/ | Mid vowel | Balanced (neither small nor large) |
| /n/ | Nasal | WARMTH, reliability, familiarity |

Strategic Layer (Semantics):

* "-ium" suffix \= periodic table element (scientific authority)  
* Familiar to corporate buyers (legitimacy)  
* Unexpected for processors (differentiation)  
* Portable across languages

Market Outcome:

* Within months: Consumers requesting "Pentium computers" specifically  
* Created category standard (Intel → Pentium association)  
* Successful line extensions: Pentium Pro, II, III (name architecture sustained)  
* 25+ year market dominance

Key Success Factor: Three-level alignment (phonetics \+ semantics \+ corporate strategy)

## **SWIFFER (P\&G, 1998): Behavioral Reframing**

Strategic Discovery:

* 25 consumer interviews revealed: Nobody likes "mopping" (viewed as moving dirt)  
* Strategic reframe: Not about "mop" → about "action" (swift, effortless motion)  
* Rejected alternatives: "Dr. Mop," "MopHit" (too descriptive/functional)

Phonetic Engineering:

| Element | Quality | Effect |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| /sw/ | Initial fricatives | Soft, flowing, light (effortless) |
| /i/ | Front vowel | Small, active, light (not heavy labor) |
| \-ER | Agent suffix | Creates verb ("one who swiffes") |

Market Impact:

* 80%+ preference in testing  
* Created new verb: "to swiff" replaced "to mop"  
* Transformed category: Behavioral shift from labor to convenience  
* Multi-line expansion: WetJet, Sweeper, Duster (name architecture sustained)  
* 25+ year market dominance

Genius Factor: Name describes behavior/experience, not product category

## **VI. CRITICAL SUCCESS FACTORS**

## **1\. Separation of Creation from Judgment (Lexicon Core)**

Problem: Large naming committees during ideation → consensus (boring)

Solution:

* Phase 1 (Generation): Ban evaluation; maximize quantity (1,000+ ideas)  
* Phase 2 (Refinement): Objective criteria only; eliminate personal preferences  
* Phase 3 (Selection): Strategic alignment \+ market fit

Outcome: Originality protected; boring consensus names eliminated

## **2\. Multi-Team Generation (Lexicon Methodology)**

Structure:

* Team A: Knows real brief (traditional approach)  
* Team B: Thinks naming a competitor (forces differentiation thinking)  
* Team C: Unrelated category (e.g., "name a bicycle" for IDE software)

Result: Multiple metaphorical territories explored; friction creates innovation

Example: Windsurf (IDE) emerged from Team C just listing "flow" concepts, not thinking about code at all

## **3\. Phonetic Strategy Alignment (Lexicon \+ Academic)**

Requirement: Phonetic profile must match brand positioning

Process:

1. Define positioning (fast? powerful? warm? innovative?)  
2. Map phonetic qualities needed (fricatives? plosives? back vowels? front vowels?)  
3. Engineer name to align phonetics with positioning  
4. Validate that automaticity works in your favor (not against)

Example: Vercel (speed positioning)

* Need: Fast-sounding phonetics  
* Deploy: V (vitality), S (precision), liquid R (flow)  
* Result: Phonetic profile communicates "momentum" automatically

## **4\. Competitive White Space Identification (Igor)**

Principle: Differentiation is positioning, not category

Process:

1. Map all competitors on Functional-to-Evocative spectrum  
2. Identify clustering (where competitors all naming similarly)  
3. Find white space (positioning category lacks)  
4. Design your name for that white space

Example: Virgin Airlines

* Competitive landscape: All functional or experiential  
* White space: No evocative airlines  
* Strategy: Move to evocative (level \+5 engagement)  
* Result: Monopoly on "exciting, different, human" positioning

## **5\. Polarization as Strength Signal (Lexicon)**

Counter-intuitive insight: Internal disagreement \= market strength

Intel Pentium Example:

* Executive committee split: "Pro Chip" (safe) vs. "Pentium" (bold)  
* CEO Andy Grove recognized split as signal  
* Grove quote: "I see the polarization here . . . that tells me there's energy for Pentium"

Other examples:

* Azure: Called "dumb name" internally; became billion-dollar platform  
* Sonos: Rejected for "not being entertainment enough"; $2.1B market cap  
* BlackBerry: Founders thought name was "crazy"; category-defining brand

Principle: If internal team comfortable, external market won't differentiate

## **6\. Linguistic Disaster Screening (Catchword Discipline)**

Goal: Pre-identify negative connotations across markets

Protocol:

* Assemble 3 native speakers per language (bilingual with English)  
* Conduct objective survey on all name candidates  
* Identify problematic associations  
* Collate risk report (which names safe for which markets)  
* Recommend market-specific strategy

Example:

* "Semen" (Portuguese cleaning product) unsuitable for English markets  
* Solution: Regional marketing or rebrand for global expansion

Discipline: Risk reduction before full trademark investment

## **7\. Multi-Dimensional Integration (Design \+ Naming)**

Sonos Precedent:

* Name: Palindromic (memorable)  
* Logo: Mirrored palindrome design  
* Product: Installer design incorporates palindrome principle  
* Brand language: Symmetry \+ balance theme

Result: Compounding brand equity; name \+ design \+ product functionality aligned
